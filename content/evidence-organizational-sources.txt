INTERPRET - What the workforce data shows

Pulled data from BLS, EEOC, and USAFacts in November 2025. Covers trends from 2013-2024. All official government sources so quality is good.

## Workforce Demographics Analysis (National Context)

### Labor Force Participation Patterns

#### Overall US Labor Force Composition (2024 BLS Data)
- **Total Labor Force:** ~168 million workers
- **Gender Distribution:**
  - Men: 53.1% of labor force
  - Women: 46.9% of labor force

- **Race/Ethnicity Distribution:**
  - White: 77.7% of labor force
  - Black or African American: 13.0% of labor force
  - Asian: 6.7% of labor force
  - Hispanic or Latino (any race): 18.8% of labor force

**Data Source:** BLS Current Population Survey, Labor Force Characteristics
**Data Quality:** High reliability - based on monthly survey of ~60,000 households

#### Representation in Management and Professional Occupations

**Management, Professional, and Related Occupations (2024):**
- **Total in Category:** ~68 million workers (40% of workforce)
- **By Race/Ethnicity:**
  - White: 78.9% (slightly higher than labor force overall)
  - Black or African American: 9.4% (lower than 13.0% labor force representation)
  - Asian: 9.5% (higher than 6.7% labor force representation)
  - Hispanic or Latino: 11.6% (lower than 18.8% labor force representation)

**Problem Connection:** Demonstrates underrepresentation of Black and Hispanic workers in professional/managerial roles relative to their labor force participation

**Data Source:** BLS Current Population Survey, Table 11: Employed persons by detailed occupation, sex, race, and Hispanic or Latino ethnicity
**Data Quality:** High - large sample size, annual averages

### Unemployment Rate Disparities

#### Unemployment Rates by Race/Ethnicity (2024 Annual Average)
- **Overall Unemployment:** 3.7%
- **White:** 3.3%
- **Black or African American:** 6.0% (1.8× white rate)
- **Asian:** 3.1%
- **Hispanic or Latino:** 4.7%

**Historical Trend (Past 10 Years):**
- Black unemployment has consistently been ~2× white unemployment rate
- Hispanic unemployment consistently 1.3-1.5× white unemployment rate
- Disparity persists across economic cycles (recession and growth periods)

**Problem Connection:** Demonstrates persistent differential employment outcomes suggesting systemic hiring barriers

**Data Source:** BLS Labor Force Statistics from Current Population Survey
**Data Quality:** High - monthly tracking with large sample

### Wage and Earnings Disparities

#### Median Weekly Earnings by Race/Ethnicity (2024 Q2)
- **All Workers:** $1,143 per week
- **White:** $1,169 per week (102% of overall median)
- **Black or African American:** $952 per week (83% of overall median)
- **Asian:** $1,488 per week (130% of overall median)
- **Hispanic or Latino:** $914 per week (80% of overall median)

**Wage Gap Analysis:**
- **Black-White Wage Gap:** Black workers earn 81% of what white workers earn
- **Hispanic-White Wage Gap:** Hispanic workers earn 78% of what white workers earn
- **Persistence:** Wage gaps have remained relatively stable over past decade

**Data Source:** BLS Usual Weekly Earnings of Wage and Salary Workers
**Data Quality:** High - quarterly data from CPS

#### Gender Wage Gap
- **Women's Earnings:** 83% of men's earnings (2024 annual median)
- **Intersectional Analysis:**
  - Black women: 69% of white men's earnings
  - Hispanic women: 62% of white men's earnings
  - Asian women: 92% of white men's earnings

**Problem Connection:** Demonstrates systemic outcome disparities suggesting discrimination in hiring, advancement, or compensation

**Data Source:** BLS Women's Earnings
**Data Quality:** High - annual analysis of CPS data

## Industry-Specific Diversity Patterns

### Technology Sector Diversity (EEOC EEO-1 Data)

**Computer and Electronic Product Manufacturing (2020 Aggregate EEO-1 Data):**

**Executive/Senior Officials and Managers:**
- White: 73.5%
- Asian: 15.8%
- Black or African American: 2.9%
- Hispanic or Latino: 4.2%

**Professionals (Software Engineers, Developers, etc.):**
- White: 56.4%
- Asian: 32.1%
- Black or African American: 3.8%
- Hispanic or Latino: 4.7%

**Problem Connection:** Severe underrepresentation of Black and Hispanic workers in tech sector, especially leadership

**Data Source:** EEOC EEO-1 Data (most recent aggregate data available)
**Data Quality:** Moderate-High - employer self-reported, not all companies file
