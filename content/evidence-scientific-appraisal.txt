## Individual Study Quality Assessment

### Study 1: Ameri & Kurtzberg (2025) - DEI Language and Disability Hiring

#### Methodological Quality
- **Study Design Appropriateness:** Excellent
  - *Justification:* Randomized controlled trial (RCT) is gold standard for testing causal effects of interventions. 2×4×2 experimental design allows isolation of DEI statement effects.
  
- **Sample Size Adequacy:** Excellent
  - *Justification:* n=1,655 participants provides excellent statistical power. Sample includes managers with actual hiring experience across industries.
  
- **Measurement Validity:** Excellent
  - *Justification:* Standardized employability rating scales, controlled experimental conditions, IRB approval, CONSORT guidelines followed.
  
- **Statistical Analysis:** Excellent
  - *Justification:* Appropriate statistical tests (p=0.032), effect size reported (Cohen's d=-0.11), controlled for confounds.

#### Risk of Bias Assessment
- **Selection Bias:** Low risk
  - Random assignment to conditions, recruited via Prolific Academic platform with screening for management experience
  
- **Information Bias:** Low risk
  - Controlled experimental exposure, standardized measurement instruments, blinded to study hypotheses
  
- **Confounding:** Well controlled
  - Randomized design controls for confounders, experimental manipulation isolates DEI statement effects
  
- **Reporting Bias:** Low risk
  - Peer-reviewed publication in Disability and Health Journal, full methodology and results reported

#### External Validity
- **Population Generalizability:** High
  - *Reasoning:* Sample includes US managers with hiring authority across industries (77% aged 25-54, balanced gender). Directly matches target population for organizational DEI interventions.
  
- **Setting Generalizability:** Medium-High
  - *Reasoning:* Online experimental setting simulates real hiring decisions but lacks full organizational context. Trade-off between experimental control and real-world complexity.
  
- **Time Relevance:** High
  - *Reasoning:* Published January 2025, addresses current DEI landscape and disability hiring practices. Very recent and timely.

## Systematic Biases and Limitations

### Publication Bias
Potential concern for Hardy et al. meta-analysis—studies showing no bias effects may be less likely to be published. However, authors address this with sensitivity analyses and note conservative bias estimates.

### Geographic Bias
Both studies focus on United States contexts. Findings may not generalize to international settings with different legal/cultural DEI contexts. This limitation is acceptable given research focus on US organizations.

### Industry Bias
Neither study funded by industry with vested interests. Both conducted by academic researchers. Hardy et al. uses hypothetical Fortune 500 scenarios (not actual company data), reducing industry influence.

### Temporal Bias
Ameri & Kurtzberg measures immediate experimental effects—unknown whether DEI statement effects persist over time. Hardy et al. models long-term cumulative effects but based on short-term experimental data extrapolation.

## Evidence Gaps

### Critical Evidence Gaps
- Long-term persistence of DEI language effects beyond immediate hiring decisions
- Real-world implementation fidelity when organizations adopt "heartfelt" DEI statements
- Intersectionality—how bias mechanisms interact across multiple marginalized identities (disability + gender + race)
- Post-hire outcomes (retention, performance, advancement) not just initial hiring decisions

### Context-Specific Needs
- Industry-specific evidence—do effects vary by sector?
- Organization size effects—do interventions work differently in small vs large organizations?
- Implementation process research—how organizations successfully adopt meaningful DEI practices

## Implications for Decision Making

### How to Weight Scientific Evidence
Scientific evidence should carry substantial weight given high methodological quality of both studies. However:
- Complement with practitioner evidence on real-world implementation
- Supplement with organizational data on actual hiring outcomes
- Balance with stakeholder perspectives on ethical/practical feasibility

### What Research Clearly Supports
- Hiring bias against people with disabilities and women is empirically documented
- Meaningful (heartfelt) DEI language is more effective than boilerplate statements
- Even small biases have large cumulative organizational consequences
- Bias reduction interventions are financially justified

### Where Other Evidence Is Critical
- Implementation details—how to craft "heartfelt" DEI statements in practice (practitioner evidence needed)
- Organizational context—baseline diversity metrics and hiring patterns (organizational data needed)
- Stakeholder buy-in—acceptance of DEI interventions by hiring managers and affected communities (stakeholder evidence needed)
