"The language of inclusion: A randomized trial of how DEI statements influence hiring practices for people with visible and invisible disabilities"

üìä Study Overview

This randomized controlled trial examined how different types of DEI statements influence hiring managers' decisions when evaluating job candidates with disabilities. Using a rigorous experimental design, 1,655 US managers were exposed to either traditional or heartfelt DEI language before making hiring assessments.

üìã Methodology Details

Design: Randomized controlled trial (2√ó4√ó2)

Analysis: ANOVA and z-tests for proportions

Sample: n=1,655 managers with hiring experience

Context: United States, cross-industry

Ethics: IRB approved, CONSORT guidelines

RCT Experimental US-Based
üéØ Key Measurements

DEI Statements: Legal/Traditional vs Heartfelt language

Disability Types: None, visible, invisible (neurological), invisible (mental health)

Outcomes: Employability, risk perception, negotiation skills, integrity

Controls: Candidate tone (warm vs overconfident)

p=0.032 d=-0.11 n=1,655
üîç Key Findings & Implications
Primary Finding: Heartfelt DEI statements significantly improved employability ratings for candidates with disabilities compared to traditional legal language (M=5.11 vs M=4.99, p=0.032).
Disability Type Effects: Stronger positive effects for candidates with invisible disabilities, but mental health disabilities faced more stigma than neurological conditions.
Practical Implications: DEI statement language serves as internal priming device for hiring managers, not just external signaling - companies can reduce bias through authentic, heartfelt DEI messaging.
Perfect Relevance: Directly answers primary research question about how DEI statements affect hiring bias and recruitment outcomes in US organizations.
